# U.S. Tariff Authorities — Reference Overview

Short reference consumed by `skills/admin/tariff-impact-mitigation-brief.md` and `skills/admin/customs-classification-brief.md`. It describes the statutory authorities the brief skills cite when sizing exposure and ranking mitigation moves. Not a substitute for outside trade counsel or for the company's licensed customs broker on a binding ruling.

## Why this overview exists

Through 2025 and into 2026, U.S. tariff policy moved from a handful of slow-moving programs to a stack of overlapping authorities the operator has to read together. A single inbound HTS line can carry MFN duty, a Section 301 add-on, a Section 232 add-on, an IEEPA executive-order add-on, an antidumping or countervailing duty, plus quota or safeguard treatment, with each authority having its own scope rule, exclusion process, and effective-date mechanic. The mitigation brief needs a stable shorthand for what each authority does so the operator does not have to re-learn the framework on every tariff event.

The goal of this reference is to give the tariff and classification skills a consistent vocabulary and a consistent pointer to the controlling text — not to embed live rate tables (which churn) or specific exclusion lists (which the team should pull from the source on every run).

## The authorities at a glance

### Section 301 (Trade Act of 1974)

- USTR-administered remedy for foreign acts, policies, or practices that are unreasonable or discriminatory and burden U.S. commerce. The operator most often sees this as the China-origin tariff lists (List 1, 2, 3, 4A, 4B in the original action; subsequent modifications and statutory four-year reviews reshape the lists)
- Scope is by HTS subheading and country of origin. Goods that fall in scope when entered for consumption pay the Section 301 add-on rate on top of the MFN duty
- Exclusion process: USTR has from time to time opened exclusion windows; granted exclusions are HTS-specific and time-limited. The brief should always check the active USTR exclusion docket rather than rely on a cached list
- FTZ treatment matters: goods admitted to an FTZ in privileged-foreign status before the effective date generally retain pre-action treatment; goods admitted as non-privileged-foreign or after the effective date pay the new rate on entry into U.S. commerce. Drawback eligibility on Section 301 has shifted over time and is the kind of question that warrants a broker confirmation rather than a skill-level assertion

### Section 232 (Trade Expansion Act of 1962)

*Update — 2026-05-06.* The April 2026 restructuring of the steel, aluminum, and copper Section 232 program is now the live posture and warrants its own structural call-out. (See the new Section 122 subsection below for the 2026-02-20 10% temporary global add-on that now stacks on top of most Section 232 lines pending the CAFC appeal.) The April 2 proclamation, effective for goods entered for consumption on or after April 6, made three changes that materially shift duty exposure on metal articles and their derivatives: the duty now applies to the full customs value of covered articles and derivatives rather than only to the metal-content portion; the rate stack was restructured into tiers (a 50% rate on aluminum articles, steel articles, most copper articles, and derivative articles substantially made of those metals as listed in the Annex I-A category; a 25% rate on certain copper articles and select derivatives in the Annex I-B category; a 10% rate available where the imported derivative is made entirely with U.S.-smelted-and-cast aluminum, U.S.-melted-and-poured steel, or U.S.-mined-and-refined copper; and a 15% transitional rate through December 31, 2027 for metal-intensive industrial equipment and electrical-grid equipment); and a *de minimis* exception applies to derivative products outside HTSUS chapters 72, 73, 74, and 76 where the aggregate weight of the steel, aluminum, and copper input is less than 15% of the imported product's total weight. The mitigation brief should treat any pre-2026-04-06 metal-content basis on a Section 232 calculation as out-of-date for entries on or after that date.

*Update — 2026-05-11.* On May 6, 2026, CBP issued CSMS #68554727 introducing two retroactive technical corrections to the April 2 / April 6 proclamation that apply to entries on or after April 6, 2026. First, CBP created a new HTSUS subheading **9903.82.01** with a **0% duty rate** for articles qualifying as non-metal articles under U.S. Note 16(c) — meaning finished-goods derivatives where the metal-input weight share falls below the *de minimis* threshold now have their own explicit classification hook rather than relying solely on the exclusionary Note 16(c) language. Second, CBP clarified that **UK-origin steel with Netherlands melt and pour** treatment is classified under **HTS 9903.82.04** through **January 1, 2028**, addressing a gap in the original annex text on the melt-and-pour evidence chain for that specific origin scenario. Both corrections are retroactive to April 6, 2026 (12:01 a.m. ET). Operational implications: (a) the 9903.82.01 classification provides the affirmative HTSUS hook the broker needs to assert the 0% rate on qualifying non-metal articles — the *de minimis* weight-share calculation and U.S. Note 16(c) determination are still the operative gate, but the entry now has an explicit subheading rather than a gap in the Annex; (b) importers of UK-origin steel with Netherlands-melt-and-pour provenance need to confirm their entries from April 6 through the date of this CSMS are filed under 9903.82.04 with any necessary post-summary corrections; (c) the CSMS source (CBP CSMS #68554727) and the controlling proclamation together are the authority pair the brief should cite on any line where either correction applies.

*Update — 2026-05-18.* The Section 232 program also extended into commercial-vehicle territory in 2026. CBP issued **CSMS #68559236** on May 6, 2026 setting out the entry-filing and reporting mechanic for the 25% Section 232 duty on **Medium- and Heavy-Duty Vehicles (MHDVs)** imposed by Presidential Proclamation 10984, where the importer has received Department of Commerce approval to apply the duty exclusively to the value of the **non-U.S. content** for vehicles qualifying for preferential treatment under USMCA. Two new HTSUS classification hooks anchor the mechanic: **9903.74.03** for the **non-U.S. content portion** of an approved MHDV at the 25% additional ad valorem rate, and **9903.74.06** for the **U.S. content portion** at a **0%** additional ad valorem rate. Operational implications: (a) an approved-importer entry is split across **two entry lines** — one for the non-U.S. content under 9903.74.03 and one for the U.S. content under 9903.74.06 — and the broker has to confirm the Commerce-approval reference is on file before applying the split; (b) the special treatment applies to entries (or warehouse withdrawals for consumption) on or after **12:01 a.m. ET on November 1, 2025**, so brokers should check whether prior entries on approved MHDVs were filed correctly and whether post-summary corrections are needed; (c) MHDVs whose importer has not been approved by Commerce continue to pay the 25% duty on the **full** customs value of the vehicle under the standard MHDV classification, not the split. This is the first Section 232 expansion in the repo's reference set where the duty mechanic is **content-based with U.S.-content exemption** rather than full-customs-value or *de minimis* — the brief should flag this pattern explicitly on any MHDV lane analysis, because applying the wrong basis would either overstate the duty (if the operator forgets to apply the U.S.-content credit on an approved entry) or understate the duty (if the operator assumes Commerce approval on an unapproved entry).

*Update — 2026-06-08.* A **June 1, 2026 proclamation further adjusting the steel / aluminum / copper Section 232 regimes** (amending the April Proclamation 11021 structure) took effect **June 8, 2026** and runs as a temporary overlay through **December 31, 2027**, after which covered goods revert to the standard Proclamation 11021 rates. CBP issued the implementing guidance on **June 5, 2026 in CSMS #68855869**, standing up new Chapter 99 headings **9903.82.20–9903.82.26** for entries on or after June 8, 2026 and before January 1, 2028. Five operator-relevant moves sit inside this adjustment, each of which can shift a derivative line's rate basis: (a) **U.S.-origin-metal threshold lowered from 95% to 85%** — the "made entirely from American metal" test that gates the reduced U.S.-content rate now requires that at least 85% (down from 95%) of the article's metal content be melted-and-poured (steel), smelted-and-cast (aluminum / copper) in the United States, applied on headings 9903.82.06, .07, .08, .23, and .24, with the requirements **cumulative** across subdivisions where an article falls under more than one; the lower bar widens U.S.-content-rate eligibility on downstream derivatives and is worth re-checking on any lane where the operator's supplier was close to but under the old 95% line; (b) a **new Annex I-C transitional structure** for certain aluminum and steel articles — 25% standard, with lower rates available for trade-deal-country imports, USMCA-qualifying goods from Canada and Mexico, and products meeting the 85% U.S.-metal threshold; (c) **agricultural equipment** (combines, harvesters) and **residential HVAC** systems and components moved from the 25% derivative rate down to the **15% transitional rate**; (d) **mobile industrial equipment** (bulldozers, forklifts) eligible for the **15% rate only when imported from a trade-deal partner country** (EU, UK, Japan, South Korea, Argentina, Ecuador, El Salvador, Guatemala, Liechtenstein, Switzerland, Taiwan, Canada, Mexico) — non-trade-deal-origin imports of the same goods stay at 25%; (e) **new derivative articles brought into scope** — aluminum lithographic plates (3701.30.00) and steel racks (9403.20.0075, 9403.20.0082, 9403.99.9040) are now subject to the applicable derivative duty. The USMCA derivative-steel mechanic on the new headings repeats the content-split pattern flagged in the 2026-05-18 MHDV update: **9903.82.20** (25% on the non-U.S. content, plus any U.S. content exceeding 40% of value) and **9903.82.21** (0% on U.S. content up to 40% of entered value) require a **two-line entry** for derivative steel from Canada and Mexico, with SPI code "S" and matching Chapter 1–97 HTS and country-of-origin on both lines. The brief should re-pin any Section 232 derivative-line analysis to the **June 8, 2026** effective date, treat the 85% threshold as the current U.S.-content gate (not 95%), and flag the 9903.82.20 / .21 two-line USMCA mechanic the same way it flags the MHDV split — applying the wrong content basis over- or under-states the duty. The controlling June 1 proclamation and CSMS #68855869 are the authority pair to cite; filing instructions for headings 9903.82.18 and 9903.82.19 are still pending a later CBP message.

- Commerce-led national-security investigation; tariff or quota remedies imposed by presidential proclamation. The operator most often sees this on steel, aluminum, copper, derivative products of those metals, and from time to time on autos and parts, semiconductors, or critical minerals depending on the active proclamations
- Scope is by HTS subheading and country of origin. Country-specific exemptions, tariff-rate quotas, and global versus origin-specific treatment have changed multiple times — the controlling proclamation and the latest CBP CSMS message are the sources to cite
- The post-April-2026 structure makes three operator-relevant determinations explicit on each line: (a) which Annex (I-A vs. I-B) the article or derivative falls in, (b) whether the U.S.-smelted-or-melted-or-mined evidence chain supports the 10% rate, (c) whether the metal-input weight share supports the *de minimis* exception. The brief should require the controlling-Annex citation and the evidence basis on the line rather than assume
- Derivative-product expansions remain a recurring trap, now with additional surface area: the operator can be importing a finished product not on the original list, but the April 2026 expansion brought a much wider range of derivatives into scope at the full-customs-value basis. The brief should always check whether a derivative expansion has been issued since the last cached run, and pin the rate basis (full customs value vs. metal-content) to the controlling proclamation date

### Section 201 (Trade Act of 1974)

- ITC-investigated safeguard, presidential proclamation. Less frequently active than 301 or 232, but when it is (washing machines, solar cells / modules historically), it imposes a tariff-rate quota or an absolute quota with annual liberalization. Country exclusions and the over-quota / in-quota rate split are both relevant

### Section 122 (Trade Act of 1974)

*Added — 2026-05-18.* Section 122 authorizes the president to impose temporary import duties of up to 15% (or quantitative restrictions) for a maximum of 150 days to address large and serious U.S. balance-of-payments deficits, after which any extension requires congressional action. It is a historically dormant authority that was reactivated for the first time in 2026.

- Live posture as of this overview. By **Presidential Proclamation 11012 (February 20, 2026)**, the administration invoked Section 122 to impose a **10% temporary global tariff** (an *ad valorem* add-on) on most U.S. imports, with the rate carried on a new Chapter 99 HTSUS line — **9903.03.01** — that the broker reports alongside the underlying HTS classification on most entries. Specific commodity, country, and end-use carve-outs are governed by the proclamation text and CBP guidance; the broker bulletin and CBP CSMS are the source of truth on the live exclusion list rather than this overview
- **Court status — moving.** On **May 7, 2026**, the U.S. Court of International Trade ruled the Section 122 tariffs **unlawful** in a 2-1 opinion, finding that the statutory conditions for invoking Section 122 were not satisfied and that the proclamation reflected an overly expansive reading of the delegated authority. Permanent injunctive relief was limited to the prevailing plaintiffs (the two named importers and the State of Washington). On **May 12, 2026**, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit (CAFC) entered an **administrative stay**, suspending the CIT's order while the government's appeal proceeds. The operator-side consequence as of this overview: **Section 122 duties remain in effect for all non-plaintiff importers**, and CBP continues to assess and collect the 10% rate on the 9903.03.01 line, but the legal posture is volatile and the eventual outcome may result in refund eligibility for entries paid under protest
- **Operational guidance.** (a) The brief should treat any inbound HTS line for non-excluded merchandise as carrying the Section 122 add-on on top of MFN duty and on top of any Section 301 / 232 / AD/CVD that also applies on the line, until CBP guidance changes; (b) entries should be filed under 9903.03.01 with the Section 122 add-on **paid**, not withheld, regardless of the CIT decision — only the named plaintiffs are out of scope of collection at this writing; (c) importers that want to preserve refund rights in the event the CAFC affirms the CIT should consider filing **protests under 19 U.S.C. § 1514** within the 180-day window from liquidation and tracking entry-and-liquidation dates on Section-122-affected lines, treating the analysis as analogous to the IEEPA post-ruling refund posture (see IEEPA section). The broker is the source of truth on the protest mechanic; this overview frames the posture
- **Stacking.** Section 122 stacks on top of MFN duty and on top of other authorities (Section 301, Section 232, AD/CVD, IEEPA-successor duties) unless the controlling proclamation or CBP CSMS expressly carves out an interaction. FTA preference (USMCA, KORUS, etc.) does **not** automatically erase the Section 122 add-on; the proclamation text controls. The brief should not assume any cross-authority erasure
- **Cadence.** Because Section 122 is statutorily capped at 150 days absent congressional action, the live posture has a built-in expiry track: either Congress extends, the administration lets the authority lapse, the CAFC stays or affirms the CIT, or a successor authority replaces it. Any of those events warrants a structural update to this section

### IEEPA (International Emergency Economic Powers Act)

*Update — 2026-04-28.* The Supreme Court ruled in February 2026 that IEEPA does not clearly authorize the imposition of duties on imports, holding that the statute permits the president to regulate imports during a declared emergency but that the imposition of tariffs is not within the authority IEEPA grants. The operator-side consequence is two-track. First, IEEPA-based tariffs paid on entries during the period the executive orders were in force are now in a refund-eligibility posture; the broker bulletin and CSMS are the source of truth on the refund-claim mechanic, the entry-by-entry eligibility window, and the timing. Second, the administration has begun replacing the IEEPA cover with Section 232 (steel, aluminum, copper, derivative-product expansions) and with newly-initiated Section 301 actions, with the operative dates and scope language differing materially from the prior IEEPA EOs they substitute for. The mitigation brief should treat any pre-2026-02-20 analysis that assumed IEEPA was an active stacking authority as out-of-date and re-pin the analysis to whatever Section 232 / 301 / other authority is currently doing the work on the lane.

- IEEPA remains a real statutory authority for sanctions, asset freezes, and other emergency economic measures. The 2026 ruling narrowed it specifically on the duty-imposition use, not on the broader statutory framework
- *Update — 2026-05-06.* The pre-ruling-entries refund pathway has now stood up an actual operator workflow. CBP launched the **Consolidated Administration and Processing of Entries (CAPE)** tool in ACE on April 20, 2026 as the Phase 1 channel for IEEPA refund claims. Phase 1 covers approximately 63% of affected entries — specifically, unliquidated entries and entries within the 90-day voluntary reliquidation window — and does not require filing a case with the Court of International Trade. Only the importer of record (IOR) or the licensed customs broker who filed the entry can file a CAPE Declaration, and a single Declaration can include up to 9,999 entries that the broker filed on behalf of various IORs. Valid refunds are generally issued within 60–90 days of CAPE Declaration acceptance unless a compliance review intervenes. As of late April 2026, brokers and importers had submitted approximately 75,300 CAPE Declarations covering more than 11.2 million entries. Liquidated-and-out-of-window entries fall outside Phase 1 and continue to depend on the protest / reconciliation / post-summary correction / court pathway analysis. The mitigation brief should treat the IEEPA refund pathway as **CAPE-Phase-1 vs. out-of-window** as the first triage rather than as a generic "broker question," and should pin the entry-status determination (unliquidated, within-90-day-reliquidation, liquidated-and-out-of-window) on each affected entry before recommending a pathway. The CSMS bulletins (68315804 introduction, 68340863 update, 68396594 available-now) are the source of truth on the mechanic; this overview is the framing
- *Update — 2026-05-25.* As of the May 12, 2026 closed status conference, CBP has not published a Phase-2 CSMS or a public scope/timing statement. Trade-press analysis as of mid-May 2026 anchors a working expectation that Phase 2 will not stand up before Q3 2026, after CBP processes the Phase-1 backlog and absorbs the first ~90 days of operational learnings; Phase 2 is expected to cover reconciliation entries (Entry Type 09), drawback claims referencing IEEPA-duty entries, and historically liquidated entries that fall outside the Phase-1 90-day window. The CBP guidance to hold reconciliation filings unless the reconciliation deadline is within 30 days (so the CAPE Declaration can be filed and processed first) is the live procedural posture the brief should cite when an out-of-window IEEPA entry shows up on a reconciliation candidate list. None of this is a CSMS-published mechanic; it remains a watch item, not a build, until CBP issues Phase-2 guidance
- *Update — 2026-06-01.* The government filed a **notice of appeal in V.O.S. Selections, Inc. v. United States on May 29, 2026**, signaling it will challenge the U.S. Court of International Trade's order requiring CBP to refund IEEPA duties on a **nationwide / universal** basis (rather than only to the named plaintiff importers). The CIT's universal-refund injunction was the structural authority underlying the CAPE Phase-1 broad-population eligibility; the government's appeal asks the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit (CAFC) to narrow the refund population to the named plaintiffs of V.O.S. Selections and the companion cases. The reissued CIT order set the government's appeal deadline at approximately **June 7, 2026**. **Operator-side posture as of this overview**: (a) CAPE Phase 1 continues to process Declarations on the current population basis — the appeal does not stay the refund pathway; (b) the **scope** of who is ultimately refund-eligible could narrow on appeal, so the IEEPA refund posture is now on a two-track watch (the original SCOTUS ruling on IEEPA-as-duty-authority is final; the CIT-ordered nationwide refund mechanism is on appeal); (c) brokers and importers should continue to file CAPE Declarations for eligible entries on the working assumption the current population basis holds, while preserving the **protest / reconciliation / 19 U.S.C. § 1514** backstop on entries where the CAPE pathway might narrow; (d) any importer that has not yet filed a CAPE Declaration on Phase-1-eligible entries should treat the June 7 appeal-filing date and any subsequent CAFC stay or expedited briefing schedule as the live cadence anchor rather than the Q3 2026 Phase-2 timing. The brief should not treat the appeal as having moved the Phase-1 refund mechanic; it has moved the durability of the universal scope. The CSMS bulletins and the CAFC docket are the source of truth on any procedural change; this overview is the framing
- *Update — 2026-06-08.* The appeal posture from the prior cycle sharpened this week into a specific **fight over finally liquidated entries** — entries that have liquidated and are past the 90-day voluntary-reliquidation window (generally more than ~80 days since liquidation), which is the population CBP cannot reach through the ordinary liquidation process without a court directive. The structural split that now matters for triage: **CAPE Phase 1 keeps working for unliquidated and recently-liquidated entries** (within the reliquidation window) — and it is working at scale, with CBP reporting roughly **$85 billion** in accepted refund claims and on the order of **$20.6 billion** already disbursed as of late May, representing more than half of all IEEPA duties submitted through CAPE — but **finally liquidated entries are the contested zone**. In a May 27 order the CIT directed the government to show cause why it should not be compelled to immediately liquidate / reliquidate all IEEPA-affected entries and ordered in-person testimony from the CBP Commissioner; the government's May 29 filing made its litigating position explicit: (1) once an entry is **finally liquidated**, CBP has no authority to reliquidate or refund without a court order, and (2) the universal injunction exceeds the court's authority under **Trump v. CASA, Inc.** (the 2025 Supreme Court decision limiting relief to non-litigants). The government has signaled it will **not object to refunds on finally liquidated entries where a court specifically orders them** — which is precisely why an importer with finally-liquidated exposure may need to **be a litigant** to recover. **Operator-side posture**: (a) for unliquidated / within-window entries, nothing changes — continue filing CAPE Declarations; (b) for **finally liquidated entries (older than ~80 days)**, the operator should prepare to file an importer-specific refund action rather than wait on a universal-injunction outcome, because if the government prevails on the CASA scope argument, only litigants recover on that population; (c) the § 1514 protest backstop remains the most conservative hedge as an entry approaches the 180-day-from-liquidation line, **but with a caveat now explicit in the government's position** — it treats finally liquidated entries as nonprotestable, and openly protested entries are currently CAPE-ineligible, so the protest-vs-CAPE election has to be made deliberately per entry rather than filed reflexively. Live cadence anchors this cycle: the test-case (Euro-Notions Florida, Inc. v. United States) appeal deadline (June 6), the Commissioner's scheduled CIT testimony (June 9), and CBP's next court status update (June 10) — any of which can move the finally-liquidated-entries mechanic. The CSMS bulletins, the CBP IEEPA-duty-refunds page, and the CIT / CAFC dockets are the source of truth; this overview is the framing
- For post-ruling entries, the brief should pin scope to the specific Section 232 proclamation, Section 301 USTR notice, AD/CVD order, or other authority that supplies the live rate. The "IEEPA-stack" language that appeared in 2025-and-early-2026 mitigation analyses should be replaced with the specific successor authority rather than carried as a placeholder
- Stacking treatment under the successor authorities is governed by the controlling proclamation and CSMS guidance. Section 232 and Section 301 traditionally stack on top of MFN duty; cross-action interactions (Section 232 on top of Section 301 on the same line, derivative-product overlap) need to be checked against the specific text rather than assumed

### Antidumping and Countervailing Duties (Title VII, Tariff Act of 1930)

- Commerce determines dumping margin or subsidy rate; ITC determines material injury; orders are HTS-and-producer specific, with rates that are reviewed annually (administrative review) and that can be retroactively assessed on entries within the period of review
- The operator's exposure on AD/CVD is materially different from Section 301 / 232 because final liability is set at liquidation, often years after entry. Cash-deposit rates at entry are not final. This timing reality should be flagged in any mitigation brief that touches an AD/CVD-covered HTS

### USMCA and other FTAs

- Origin-conferring rules, not tariff-imposing rules. USMCA preference can erase MFN duty when the rule of origin (tariff-shift, regional value content, or specific process) is met and a valid certification is on file. USMCA preference does **not** automatically erase Section 232 or Section 301 add-ons; the interaction depends on the proclamation
- The operator should not assume an FTA preference removes IEEPA-tariff exposure either; recent EO text has been variable on this point. Always check the controlling EO

### Miscellaneous Tariff Bill (MTB), GSP, and other programs

- MTB is a periodic congressional act granting temporary duty suspensions or reductions on specified products. Active windows are statutory — when the program is lapsed, the suspension does not apply
- GSP (Generalized System of Preferences) lapses and renews on its own schedule. When lapsed, retroactive refund is sometimes provided when the program is reauthorized; when active, the country-eligibility list and the product-eligibility list both matter

## The mitigation menu, structured

The mitigation brief evaluates moves against this menu. Each move is real, but each has a feasibility window and a program prerequisite that the brief should check rather than assume.

### Duty deferral and elimination

- **Foreign Trade Zone (FTZ)** — Activated zones admit foreign merchandise without duty until entered for consumption. Re-export from the zone avoids U.S. duty entirely. Weekly entry treatment can compress MPF. Privileged-foreign status locks classification and rate at admission, which is sometimes valuable around a tariff-effective date and sometimes not
- **Bonded warehouse (Class 3 / 4 / 7)** — Defers duty up to five years; useful for timing, less useful for rate avoidance unless a known rate change is coming
- **Duty drawback** — Refund of duty on imported merchandise that is subsequently exported (1313(j)(1) unused-merchandise direct-identification, 1313(j)(2) substitution unused, 1313(p) petroleum, 1313(q) wine; manufactured-merchandise drawback under 1313(a) and (b)). Eligibility for drawback on Section 301, Section 232, and IEEPA tariffs has been treated differently across actions — always confirm with the broker against the controlling text

### Valuation moves

- **First-sale-for-export** — Lower dutiable value where a multi-tier sale exists and statutory conditions are met. Requires evidentiary support that survives CBP scrutiny: the first-sale transaction must be a bona fide sale destined for export to the U.S., at arm's length, with adequate documentation. Not a paper-only restructure
- **Unbundling non-dutiable charges** — International freight, insurance, and certain post-importation services and royalties may be excluded from transaction value when properly documented and separately stated. Common audit area; the brief should treat any "unbundling" claim as requiring documentary support, not just an invoice line split

### Origin and classification moves

- **Substantial transformation / re-source** — Shifting production to a non-affected country can change the country of origin and remove the action's scope. Real moves take months to years and depend on the product's production-step sensitivity
- **Tariff engineering** — Legitimate product modification that shifts the classification to a different HTS at a lower rate. Bona fide engineering is recognized by CBP; sham modification is not. This belongs in `customs-classification-brief.md`, not the mitigation brief
- **Component-vs-finished split** — Importing components subject to a lower or no tariff, with assembly in the U.S. or in an FTZ. Has working-capital and labor-cost implications that the brief should surface, not gloss

### Procedural and timing moves

- **Exclusion request** — When an exclusion process is open (USTR Section 301; Commerce 232 derivative-product process), file with product specificity and economic justification. Outcomes are HTS-specific and time-limited
- **Pull-forward / hold-back of entries** — Bracketing an effective date is sometimes legitimate (transit on the water before effective date is treated under pre-action rate where the EO so provides), and is sometimes not (artificial entry-date manipulation is not). The brief should describe the rule under the controlling text, not propose a workaround that reads as evasion
- **Bonded vs. consumption entry on the same shipment** — Bonded admission to wait out a known short-window tariff event has cash-flow merit when warehouse fees are less than the duty exposure for the wait period

### Pricing and contract moves

- **Pass-through to the customer** — Structural decision driven by the contract's pass-through stance, not by the brief. The brief frames the conversation; the rate change is finalized in the company's pricing system

## How the mitigation brief should use this reference

The brief should:

1. Pin every authority cited in the analysis to the section of this overview, with a one-line callout of which controlling document (EO, proclamation, USTR notice, CBP CSMS) supplies the live rate and scope
2. Sanity-check stacking — if the analysis claims a move (e.g., USMCA preference, drawback) erases an authority's add-on, the brief should require the controlling-document language that supports that claim, not infer it
3. Flag the common traps explicitly: derivative-list expansion under 232; AD/CVD final-liability timing; IEEPA scope and stacking variability; exclusion-list expiration; FTZ status election timing
4. Route HTS-classification questions to `customs-classification-brief.md` rather than re-doing the work

## Scope limits

This overview is a working operator's reference, not legal advice. It does not cover state tax treatment, Buy America / Buy American Act procurement rules, end-use programs (TIB, Chapter 98), foreign tariff regimes, or sanctions screening (which sits in its own KB area). Any move that depends on a binding ruling, an FTZ activation, a drawback program registration, or a USMCA certification belongs to the broker and to counsel, not to this skill.
