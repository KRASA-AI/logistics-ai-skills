# Broker Carrier-Vetting Liability — Operator Reference

> **Posture:** This is a working operator's reference, not legal advice. The liability framing in this file changes how the carrier-vetting skill cluster (`carrier-fraud-screening-brief.md`, `carrier-identity-change-monitor-brief.md`, `carrier-insider-risk-brief.md`, `pickup-identity-verification-brief.md`) should be read — fraud-loss prevention is no longer the only driver of vetting depth; **negligent-hiring exposure under state tort law** is now a parallel, cumulative driver. Any specific claim posture, defense, or settlement decision belongs to in-house counsel and the broker's primary auto-and-cargo insurer, not to a brief skill.

## Why this reference exists

Through the prior cycles, the repo's carrier-vetting skills were framed around **fraud loss** — chameleon carriers, double-brokering, impersonation, fictitious pickups. That framing is correct and remains the day-to-day operator workflow. But the legal backdrop the cluster sits inside changed structurally on **May 14, 2026** when the U.S. Supreme Court decided **Montgomery v. Caribe Transport II, LLC** 9-0, holding that **state-law negligent-hiring claims against freight brokers are not preempted by the Federal Aviation Administration Authorization Act (FAAAA)** because such claims fall within the FAAAA's safety exception preserving state authority to regulate safety with respect to motor vehicles. The federal-preemption shield that brokers and many trial courts had treated as a near-categorical defense to crash-related negligent-hiring suits is gone. The operator-side consequence is that the vetting record the broker keeps now serves a **dual purpose**: (a) the original fraud-loss-prevention purpose the cluster was built around, and (b) the litigation-defense purpose of demonstrating reasonable care in carrier selection at the moment the load was tendered. The brief should write the vetting record so it survives both readings.

This reference frames the post-Montgomery vetting posture for the cluster. It does not replace the cluster — the layered Pass / Flag / Fail mechanics in `carrier-fraud-screening-brief.md` continue to do the operational work. It adds the **legal-defense overlay** that the existing layers should be tuned against, and pins the new cross-references the cluster should consume.

## What Montgomery actually held (operator framing)

The plaintiff in Montgomery was a motorist whose tractor-trailer was struck by a truck operated by Caribe Transport II on an Illinois interstate; the plaintiff lost a leg and sustained other permanent injuries. The plaintiff's suit named the freight broker (C.H. Robinson) on a state-law negligent-hiring theory, alleging the broker should have known the carrier carried a **conditional** FMCSA safety rating and had documented deficiencies in driver-qualification, hours-of-service, inspection-and-maintenance, and crash-rate dimensions at the time the load was tendered. The legal question was whether the FAAAA's express preemption of state laws "related to a price, route, or service" of a broker preempts the negligent-hiring claim. The Court held it does not — the safety exception in 49 U.S.C. § 14501(c)(2)(A) saves the claim from preemption because the duty of care being enforced is a generally applicable safety duty as applied to motor vehicles.

The operator framing the cluster should adopt:

- **Preemption defense narrows materially.** The pre-Montgomery posture in many jurisdictions treated negligent-hiring suits as preempted at the motion-to-dismiss stage. That posture is foreclosed at the federal-floor level; whether a specific state-law claim survives now depends on the state's own substantive negligence-law framework (duty, breach, causation, damages) rather than on federal preemption
- **The negligent-hiring duty is an "ordinary duty of care."** The standard the broker is being measured against is what a reasonable broker in the same position would have done, not a heightened or strict-liability standard. The vetting record's job is to make the reasonable-care answer visible in the documents
- **The exposure is state-by-state.** Brokers operating in 48 / 49 / 50 states are now exposed to 48 / 49 / 50 different state negligence regimes on the same vetting decision. The broker's vetting program is the federal-floor uniformity layer the broker controls; the state-law variance is the legal-defense overlay that varies
- **The case returns to the lower courts on the merits.** Montgomery decided preemption only; the broker can still defend by showing the carrier-selection decision was reasonable on the data available at tender. That defense rides on the vetting record

## What "reasonable care" looks like in the vetting record

A vetting record is "reasonable" not because it follows a single checklist verbatim, but because it shows the operator answered four questions before tendering, and re-answered them on a defensible cadence after onboarding:

1. **Did the carrier carry active operating authority for the commodity, equipment, and lane on the day the load was tendered?** Active common / contract / household-goods authority appropriate to the commodity; MCS-150 within the agency's update window; insurance on file on SAFER consistent with the COI in the broker's file; no out-of-service order in force
2. **Did the carrier's safety profile, on the data the FMCSA made available, support the selection?** FMCSA safety rating (Satisfactory / Conditional / Unsatisfactory) noted with the date the broker pulled it; CSA Safety Measurement System (SMS) BASIC percentiles (Unsafe Driving, Hours-of-Service Compliance, Driver Fitness, Vehicle Maintenance, Crash Indicator, Controlled Substances and Alcohol) checked against the broker's policy thresholds; any BASIC above the intervention threshold either disqualifying or accompanied by a written, specific rationale for proceeding
3. **Did the broker confirm financial-responsibility evidence that survives a post-event audit?** COI in file with limits at or above the broker's policy minimum (and at or above the customer's contractual minimum where applicable); endorsements consistent with the broker's standard (additional insured, waiver of subrogation, primary-and-non-contributory, notice-of-cancellation cadence); broker-of-record on the COI verified by callback to the agency rather than only from the PDF
4. **Did the broker re-check the carrier on a documented cadence after onboarding?** A clean snapshot on the day of onboarding is not a defense for a crash six months later if the carrier's safety profile deteriorated and the broker continued tendering without re-screening. Authority revocations, insurance lapses, out-of-service orders, conditional / unsatisfactory rating changes, and significant SMS deterioration on the BASICs the broker monitors should trigger an alert, an automatic hold on new tenders to that carrier, and a documented re-screen before any further tendering

The vetting record that answers these four questions with date-stamped evidence is the record the broker can put in front of a jury years after the fact. The record that answers them in a templated "approved" check-box without the supporting data is the record that loses on summary judgment.

## How this overlays on the existing carrier-vetting cluster

The four briefs in the cluster were built for fraud-loss prevention. Each continues to do that work; the post-Montgomery overlay adds a legal-defense reading on top of the existing fraud-flag reading.

- **`carrier-fraud-screening-brief.md`** — The layered Pass / Flag / Fail framework already captures authority, insurance, identity, and pattern indicators. Post-Montgomery, the brief should additionally pin (a) the FMCSA safety rating (Satisfactory / Conditional / Unsatisfactory) with the date pulled, (b) the BASIC percentiles for each measured BASIC with the date pulled, (c) a written rationale on any Proceed disposition where a BASIC is over the intervention threshold or the safety rating is Conditional. The Proceed-With-Conditions language already in the brief is the natural home for that rationale. **Block** dispositions should additionally cite the safety-rating or BASIC basis when those are the driver, rather than only the fraud-pattern basis
- **`carrier-identity-change-monitor-brief.md`** — Change-of-ownership monitoring continues as a fraud control. Post-Montgomery, the brief should add a re-screen trigger when a change-of-ownership event coincides with a deterioration in the FMCSA safety rating or in any monitored BASIC percentile, and should pin the legal-defense framing in the rationale: a broker that continues to tender to a carrier whose safety rating deteriorated after a change-of-ownership without a documented re-screen has the worst of both worlds (the fraud-loss exposure and the negligent-hiring exposure)
- **`carrier-insider-risk-brief.md`** — The clean-record-carrier risk evaluation framing continues. Post-Montgomery, the brief should distinguish between a clean-on-paper carrier with an insider-risk pattern (the original brief's subject) and a clean-on-paper carrier whose safety profile is starting to drift on the BASICs (a separate negligent-hiring-exposure pattern). Both warrant a documented review; the legal-defense rationale is different for each
- **`pickup-identity-verification-brief.md`** — Pickup-window identity verification continues. Post-Montgomery, the brief should note that a verified pickup does not retroactively reasonable-care the carrier-selection decision made at tender; the vetting record at tender is the record that matters for the negligent-hiring defense, not the pickup-time verification. The two layers protect against different exposures (pickup verification protects against fraud-loss at the moment of pickup; tender-time vetting protects against negligent-hiring exposure across the life of the load and beyond)

## The re-screening cadence the cluster should pin

The cluster has historically treated re-screening as a discretionary control. Post-Montgomery, re-screening becomes the bridge between a defensible onboarding snapshot and a defensible mid-relationship tender decision. A workable cadence the briefs can reference:

- **Event-driven re-screen** — Any of: authority status change in SAFER, MCS-150 lapse beyond the agency window, insurance lapse, COI expiration without renewal, change-of-ownership event, out-of-service order, FMCSA safety-rating change, BASIC percentile crossing an intervention threshold the broker is monitoring, claim or near-miss the broker's own claims desk logged on the carrier. Each event triggers a documented re-screen before the next tender
- **Time-driven re-screen** — On a 90-day or 180-day cadence appropriate to the lane risk and load value the carrier is tendering, even with no event triggers. The cadence should be tighter on high-value, hazmat, controlled-substance, and cross-border lanes
- **Holds and unholds** — The TMS should put a carrier on hold on any event-driven trigger and require a documented unhold (with the re-screen evidence attached) before further tenders. The hold itself is the broker's defense against a "we kept tendering after the deterioration" theory

## Documentation that survives a discovery request

The vetting record's audience is no longer only the compliance officer and the insurer; it is also opposing counsel in a state-court personal-injury or wrongful-death action. The cluster's briefs should write the record to that audience:

- **Date-stamp every data pull** — SAFER snapshot date, COI receipt date, BASIC pull date, safety-rating pull date. A vetting record without dates is a vetting record that cannot be located in time relative to the tender
- **Preserve the source artifact, not just the derivative summary** — A PDF of the SAFER snapshot, the COI PDF as received from the agency, an exported BASIC report, are all auditable. A summary that says "authority verified" without the underlying snapshot is the record that loses on cross-examination
- **Pin the rationale on any Proceed disposition over an indicator** — Proceed-With-Conditions and Proceed-over-flag dispositions should carry a written rationale that names the indicator, the mitigating factors, and the decision owner. The rationale is the record that converts a flag into a reasonable-care decision rather than a missed flag
- **Hold-the-relationship records, not just hold-the-load records** — Every hold on a carrier (event-driven or otherwise) should be on the carrier's file, not only on the affected load. A pattern of holds with documented unholds reads as active vigilance; a single hold with no unhold record reads as an abandoned-in-place flag
- **Re-screening records aggregate** — A carrier's file should show the original onboarding screen plus every event-driven and time-driven re-screen, in chronological order. The pattern of re-screens lets the broker reconstruct, at any point in the relationship, the carrier's vetting status on the specific tender date in question and the rationale that supported the proceed decision on that record

## What this reference deliberately does not cover

- **The substantive elements of state negligence law** (duty, breach, causation, damages, comparative fault, vicarious liability) — these are state-by-state and belong to counsel, not to a vetting brief
- **The broker's primary auto-and-cargo insurance program** (errors-and-omissions, contingent auto liability, contingent cargo, umbrella tower structure, certificate-holder requirements) — these are insurance-program decisions that belong to the broker's risk-management and brokerage E&O carrier, not to a vetting brief
- **The shipper-contract indemnification posture** — pass-through indemnification, hold-harmless language, and additional-insured requirements between the shipper, the broker, and the carrier are contract-drafting decisions that belong to in-house counsel
- **Settlement strategy on any specific claim** — the vetting record is the input to the defense; the defense strategy itself is counsel's call
- **State-by-state vetting checklists** — the federal-floor vetting program is the broker's uniformity layer; a 50-state checklist would either be too thin (the lowest common denominator) or rot quickly (the per-state variance changes faster than a static reference can track)

## Source-of-truth pointers

The Court's opinion and the underlying statutory text are the controlling documents; trade-press summaries inform timing and operator framing but should never substitute for the controlling text on a specific claim posture. Pin per-citation:

- Montgomery v. Caribe Transport II, LLC, 608 U.S. ___ (May 14, 2026), Slip Op. (the controlling opinion)
- 49 U.S.C. § 14501(c)(1) (FAAAA express preemption clause) and § 14501(c)(2)(A) (safety exception)
- 49 C.F.R. Part 385 (FMCSA safety fitness rating procedures)
- FMCSA SAFER system and the Compliance, Safety, Accountability (CSA) program's Safety Measurement System (SMS) public methodology document for each BASIC
- The broker's own underwriting and risk-management policy (BASIC intervention thresholds the broker enforces; COI minimums; rating thresholds at which a Proceed becomes Proceed-With-Conditions or Block)
- State-by-state negligence-law framework where the broker tenders loads or where the broker is sued — counsel's territory, not the brief's
